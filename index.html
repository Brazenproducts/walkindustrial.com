Overview

Walk Industrial focuses on filtration products for demanding environments: manufacturing facilities, commercial HVAC systems, woodworking shops, metal fabrication, and clean-room applications. The difference between residential and industrial filtration is substantial — industrial filters must handle higher particulate loads, longer service intervals, and stricter performance documentation requirements. A filter that works perfectly in a home furnace may collapse under the static pressure of a commercial air handler running 24/7. Buying Guide — What to Know Before You Buy

MERV Rating Selection

Match MERV to your application: MERV 8 for standard commercial HVAC. MERV 11 for offices and schools. MERV 13 for medical-adjacent and fine-particle environments. MERV 16+ for true clean-room applications. Over-specifying increases energy costs.

Pressure Drop and System Design

Every filter creates resistance (pressure drop). Higher MERV = higher pressure drop. Verify your air handler can maintain design airflow at the selected filter's rated pressure drop. Insufficient airflow damages heat exchangers and reduces system life.

Industrial vs. Residential Media

Industrial filters use thicker, more densely packed media rated for higher dust holding capacity. Using residential filters in commercial air handlers results in premature loading and frequent replacement — false economy.

Maintenance Scheduling

Industrial filters require scheduled replacement based on differential pressure measurement, not just calendar time. Install manometer gauges across filter banks to monitor pressure drop and replace when rated maximum is reached.

Frequently Asked Questions

What MERV rating do I need for my facility?

Match to application: standard offices/warehouses = MERV 8–11. Schools = MERV 11–13. Healthcare adjacent = MERV 13–16. Manufacturing with fine particle generation = MERV 13+ with pre-filters. Consult ASHRAE 62.1 for specific guidance.

How often should industrial filters be replaced?

Replace when differential pressure across the filter reaches the manufacturer's rated maximum (typically 1.0" WG for MERV 8, 0.85" WG for MERV 13). Calendar-based replacement is acceptable if you measure and have established baseline change intervals.

Can I use higher MERV filters in an existing system?

Verify your air handler's rated static pressure before upgrading MERV rating. Some older systems cannot maintain design airflow against higher pressure drop. Over-restricting airflow causes heat exchanger damage and system failure.

What's the difference between MERV and MPR ratings?

MERV (Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value) is an ASHRAE industry standard (ASHRAE 52.2). MPR (Microparticle Performance Rating) is 3M's proprietary system. MERV 11 approximately equals MPR 1000–1200.

Do industrial filters require special disposal?

Filters used in medical facilities, chemical manufacturing, or other regulated environments may require hazmat disposal. Standard HVAC filters in commercial offices and warehouses are typically landfill-acceptable — check local regulations.

What pre-filters are recommended for dusty industrial environments?

MERV 4–8 pre-filters extend the service life of downstream MERV 13+ final filters by capturing coarse particulates before they load the fine-media final filter. Pre-filter sections should change 3–5x more frequently than final filters.

How do I size industrial filters correctly?

Filter sizing is based on face velocity — the airflow rate divided by the filter face area. Target 300–500 FPM face velocity for most commercial applications. Higher face velocity reduces efficiency and increases pressure drop.

What certifications should industrial filters have?

Look for ASHRAE 52.2 test data (efficiency by particle size) and UL 900 fire resistance rating for most commercial applications. Clean-room and medical applications may require ISO 16890 or HEPA/ULPA certification.
